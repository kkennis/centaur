# Planning Content Launches

Turns a draft, memo, announcement, essay, recap, event, launch video, or product launch into a concrete brief that improves reach without diluting Paradigm's substance-first brand.

## What This Skill Does

Use this skill to help a team answer:
- What is the sharpest version of the thesis?
- Who is this actually for?
- Which channels deserve effort, and which do not?
- What supporting assets make this materially stronger?
- Who should post first, and what should happen next?
- How should the team measure success after launch?
- Is this format strong enough to repeat, or is it fighting the message?
- What creative direction would make this piece more memorable and more watchable?
- What should the team explicitly learn from this format for the next launch?

This skill supports five modes:
- `Preflight`: pressure-test the content before it ships
- `Launch Plan`: create the full launch brief and amplification plan
- `Creative Direction`: shape the concept, hook, structure, and visual treatment for a content asset, especially video or high-leverage creative packaging
- `Format Teardown`: diagnose whether a recap, summary, thread, or recurring format is likely to travel, where it loses people, and what to keep, change, or stop doing
- `Postmortem`: review what happened and recommend what to reuse next time

If the user does not specify a mode, default to `Launch Plan`.

Route into the closest mode when the ask is implicit:
- virality, recap review, recurring-series feedback, format critique, or "what should survive into the next version?" -> `Format Teardown`
- creative direction, concepting, video review, edit feedback, or packaging direction -> `Creative Direction`
- launch planning, channel mix, sequencing, or amplification -> `Launch Plan`
- pre-ship feedback on a draft -> `Preflight`
- after-action review or "what should we reuse?" -> `Postmortem`

## Response Style

Default to:
- clean inline hyperlinks instead of pasted raw URLs when links are needed
- readable prose with short, decision-oriented lists instead of fragmented bullet spam
- direct judgments about what is strong, weak, missing, or mismatched
- an explicit `Learnings To Reuse` section whenever the task involves format design, recap analysis, creative direction, or postmortem work

## Paradigm Principles

Always optimize for the right audience and durable trust, not empty reach.

Prefer:
- a sharper point of view over broader but flatter copy
- evidence, charts, examples, and proof over slogans
- native packaging for each channel over posting the same asset everywhere
- a small number of strong supporting assets over a long list of weak ones
- clear recommendations over hedged brainstorming

Avoid:
- generic B2B marketing language
- recommending channels just because they are available
- advising obvious amplification that weakens the brand
- optimizing for impressions when the real goal is qualified conversations, citations, or trust

## Inputs

Gather only the inputs that materially change the recommendation:
- the draft, link, or short description of the content
- the launch goal
- the highest-priority audiences
- timing or launch date
- accounts or people available to amplify
- assets already available: charts, screenshots, quotes, video, FAQ, data

If some fields are missing, do not stall. Make explicit assumptions and proceed.

## Workflow

### 1. Classify The Content

Determine the primary archetype:
- major announcement
- essay or thought piece
- policy paper or brief
- dashboard or data launch
- program, fellowship, or event
- product update
- social-first commentary

If two archetypes apply, name the primary one and explain the secondary one.

### 2. Define The Goal Ladder

Translate the request into a concrete goal hierarchy.

Examples:
- awareness among frontier founders
- credibility with regulators or policy staff
- qualified inbound from builders or recruits
- press pickup or podcast invitations
- citations, saves, or high-signal replies

Do not collapse all goals into one bucket. Rank them.

### 3. Define The Audience Map

Rank audiences by priority and be explicit about who the content is not for.

For each top audience, note:
- what they care about
- where they already spend attention
- what proof they need before sharing or engaging

### 4. Pressure-Test The Core Thesis

Extract and evaluate:
- the one-sentence thesis
- why now
- what is genuinely distinctive
- what proof supports the claim
- what tension or contrarian edge makes the piece memorable

If the thesis is soft, say so directly and rewrite it more sharply.

### 4.5 Diagnose The Format Mechanics

When the ask involves virality, recap quality, readability, or format performance, evaluate:
- the opening hook and whether it earns attention fast enough
- pacing, structure, and whether the format makes the audience work too hard
- how clearly the format signals novelty, stakes, or payoff
- whether the chosen container helps the idea travel or buries it
- what element is reusable versus one-off

### 5. Choose The Distribution Shape

Recommend one primary lane and up to three secondary lanes from:
- X
- LinkedIn
- blog or website
- email or newsletter
- press
- podcast
- webinar or event
- direct outreach, DM, or email

Explain why each chosen lane fits the content and audience.

Explicitly call out lanes that should not be emphasized.

### 6. Build The Asset Matrix

Sort supporting assets into:
- `Must Have`
- `Should Have`
- `Optional`

Possible assets include:
- launch copy
- thread or post variants
- chart or framework visual
- annotated screenshots
- short demo clip
- key quote or founder quote
- FAQ or reply bank
- plain-English summary
- press backgrounder
- speaker or podcast talking points

Only recommend assets that genuinely increase odds of success.

### 7. Plan The Launch Sequence

Lay out a simple sequence:
- `T-24h`
- `Launch Hour`
- `+6h`
- `+24h`
- `+72h`

Specify:
- who posts first
- what the firm account should do
- what the author or partner accounts should do
- what follow-up replies, reposts, or summaries should happen
- when to use direct outreach instead of public posting

### 8. Define Success And Failure Modes

Choose metrics tied to the actual goal, such as:
- reach to the target audience
- saves, shares, or citations
- high-signal replies
- inbound founder, regulator, or journalist conversations
- meeting requests
- podcast or speaking invites

Name likely failure modes, such as:
- strong idea, weak packaging
- too much context missing for non-crypto audiences
- no proof artifact
- all effort concentrated on one channel
- no prepared replies or follow-up asset

### 9. Produce The Right Output

Default to a `Launch Brief`.

## Channel Heuristics

Use these as default guidance, then adapt to the user's context.

### X

Best for:
- timely or technical ideas
- real-time conversation
- opinionated framing
- threads, charts, screenshots, or short clips

Recommendations:
- lead with value, not a naked link
- plan replies and follow-up posts, not just the first post
- optimize for conversation depth, not just likes

### LinkedIn

Best for:
- durable authority
- policy, business, recruiting, and operator audiences
- native summaries from a credible individual voice

Recommendations:
- prefer member-led posts when the author has real authority
- if recommending Thought Leader Ads, note the constraints: eligible formats include single image, video, event, article, and newsletter ads; polls, documents, multi-image posts, and reshares are not eligible; sponsoring requires company permissions plus creator approval

### Blog Or Website

Best for:
- canonical depth
- searchability and citation
- housing the full argument or artifact

Recommendations:
- make structure legible with sharp headings and scannable summaries
- ensure the page can support later repackaging into social, email, and press

### Email Or Newsletter

Best for:
- existing audience capture
- launch recaps and synthesis
- driving deeper consumption after social discovery

### Press, Podcasts, And Events

Use only when the claim is externally legible and genuinely newsworthy or when live explanation materially improves understanding.

### Direct Outreach

Use for:
- regulators
- priority founders
- journalists
- portfolio operators
- candidates or recruits

If targeted outreach is likely to outperform public posting for the actual goal, say so clearly.

## Archetype Playbooks

### Major Announcement

Prioritize:
- hero narrative
- concise proof points
- firm plus author sequencing
- FAQ and reply prep
- selective press if warranted

### Essay Or Thought Piece

Prioritize:
- canonical essay or blog post
- thread version
- LinkedIn-native summary
- one chart, framework, or visual that carries the argument

### Policy Paper Or Brief

Prioritize:
- plain-English summary
- stakeholder-specific framing
- strong citations
- targeted direct outreach
- press only if the timing and claim justify it

### Dashboard Or Data Launch

Prioritize:
- one surprising finding
- annotated visuals
- short demo or screenshots
- examples of how builders or researchers should use it

### Program, Fellowship, Or Event

Prioritize:
- who it is for
- social proof
- timeline and deadline clarity
- reminder ladder before the close date

### Recap Or Summary Format

Prioritize:
- a clear angle, not just a compressed transcript
- fast orientation on why the audience should care
- scannability without flattening the substance
- explicit decisions about what belongs in-link versus inline

### Video Or Motion Asset

Prioritize:
- a strong opening beat in the first few seconds
- visual proof, not just narrated claims
- format-native pacing for the intended channel
- clear cutdown opportunities for distribution after launch

## Output Format

When in `Launch Plan` mode, return a `Launch Brief` with these sections in order:

1. `Mode`
2. `Summary`
3. `Core Thesis`
4. `Audience Priorities`
5. `Primary Goal And Secondary Goals`
6. `Recommended Channels`
7. `Asset Matrix`
8. `Launch Sequence`
9. `Success Metrics`
10. `Risks And Missing Pieces`

If helpful, include short example copy blocks for the top one or two channels.

When in `Preflight` mode, keep the output shorter and focus on:
- thesis strength
- audience fit
- missing proof
- channel fit
- top three improvements before launch

When in `Creative Direction` mode, return:
- creative objective
- target audience reaction
- concept direction
- opening hook or first-beat recommendation
- structure, pacing, and visual treatment notes
- asset or cutdown recommendations
- risks and what to avoid
- learnings to reuse

When in `Format Teardown` mode, return:
- format diagnosis
- what helps it travel
- what slows it down or makes it less readable
- keep, change, and remove recommendations
- one to three experiments worth running next
- learnings to reuse

When in `Postmortem` mode, return:
- what shipped
- what worked
- what underperformed
- what surprised the team
- what to standardize next time
- learnings to reuse

## Research Guidance

If the request depends on channel behavior, audience nuance, or policy/media context that is not obvious from the draft alone, do a targeted research pass before finalizing the recommendation.

Prefer:
- official platform documentation first
- recent, credible industry research second
- direct examples only when they materially sharpen the advice

Do not pad the answer with generalized trends if the recommendation can already be made from the content and context provided.
